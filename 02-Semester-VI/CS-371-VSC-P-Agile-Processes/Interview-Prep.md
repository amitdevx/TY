# Agile Processes Interview Preparation

> [!summary] About
> Top 20+ Agile and Scrum interview questions. Essential for software engineering behavioral rounds and Project Management roles.

---

## 1. Agile Basics

> [!question] 1. What is Agile Software Development?
> Agile is a set of principles for software development under which requirements and solutions evolve through the collaborative effort of self-organizing cross-functional teams. It advocates adaptive planning, evolutionary development, early delivery, and continual improvement.

> [!question] 2. Explain the difference between Agile and Waterfall methodologies.
> - **Waterfall:** Sequential, linear process. Requirements are defined upfront. Difficult to accommodate changes once development starts. High risk if client requirements change. Testing happens at the end.
> - **Agile:** Iterative, incremental process. Requirements evolve over time. Highly adaptable to changing requirements. Testing is continuous.

> [!question] 3. What are the 4 core values of the Agile Manifesto?
> 1. Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
> 2. Working software over comprehensive documentation
> 3. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
> 4. Responding to change over following a plan

## 2. Scrum Framework

> [!question] 4. What is Scrum?
> Scrum is a lightweight, agile framework for developing, delivering, and sustaining complex products. It employs an iterative, incremental approach to optimize predictability and control risk.

> [!question] 5. Explain the role of the Scrum Master.
> The Scrum Master is a servant-leader for the Scrum Team. They:
> - Ensure Scrum is understood and enacted.
> - Remove impediments blocking the team's progress.
> - Facilitate Scrum events.
> - Shield the team from external interruptions.
> *(They are NOT a project manager or a team boss).*

> [!question] 6. What is the role of the Product Owner?
> The Product Owner is responsible for maximizing the value of the product resulting from the work of the Development Team. They:
> - Manage the Product Backlog (writing User Stories, prioritizing).
> - Are the sole voice representing the business/customer to the team.
> - Accept or reject the work delivered at the end of the Sprint.

> [!question] 7. What is a Sprint?
> A Sprint is a time-boxed iteration (usually 1-4 weeks) during which a "Done", usable, and potentially releasable product Increment is created. Sprints have consistent durations throughout a development effort.

> [!question] 8. What are the artifacts in Scrum?
> 1. **Product Backlog:** Ordered list of everything needed in the product.
> 2. **Sprint Backlog:** Set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint, plus a plan for delivering them.
> 3. **Increment:** The sum of all completed backlog items during a Sprint and all previous Sprints.

> [!question] 9. What happens in a Daily Scrum (Stand-up)?
> It's a 15-minute time-boxed event for the Development Team to synchronize activities and plan for the next 24 hours. Members answer:
> 1. What did I do yesterday that helped the team meet the Sprint Goal?
> 2. What will I do today to help the team meet the Sprint Goal?
> 3. Do I see any impediment that prevents me or the team from meeting the Sprint Goal?

## 3. Estimation & Metrics

> [!question] 10. What are User Stories?
> Short, simple descriptions of a feature told from the perspective of the person who desires the new capability, usually a user or customer.
> **Format:** "As a [type of user], I want [some goal] so that [some reason]."

> [!question] 11. What are Story Points?
> A relative unit of measure used in Agile to estimate the overall effort required to implement a User Story. It accounts for **volume of work, complexity, and risk/uncertainty**--not just time/hours.

> [!question] 12. What is Planning Poker?
> A consensus-based estimation technique where team members secretly select a card (usually Fibonacci numbers) representing their estimate for a story. Cards are revealed simultaneously, and major differences are discussed until consensus is reached.

> [!question] 13. What is Velocity?
> Velocity is a metric that measures the amount of work (in Story Points) a team can successfully complete during a single Sprint. It is used to forecast how much work the team can take on in future Sprints.

> [!question] 14. What is a Burndown Chart?
> A graphical representation of work left to do versus time. The outstanding work (or backlog) is often on the vertical axis, with time along the horizontal. It is useful for predicting when all of the work will be completed.

## 4. Kanban & DevOps

> [!question] 15. What is the difference between Scrum and Kanban?
> - **Scrum:** Uses fixed-length iterations (Sprints). Roles are defined. Work is committed at the start of the Sprint and cannot be added mid-Sprint.
> - **Kanban:** Continuous flow system. No required roles. Focuses on visualizing workflow and strictly limiting Work In Progress (WIP). Work is pulled continuously as capacity permits.

> [!question] 16. What is Test-Driven Development (TDD)?
> A software development process relying on a very short development cycle:
> 1. Write an initially failing test that defines a desired improvement or new function.
> 2. Produce the minimum amount of code to pass that test.
> 3. Refactor the new code to acceptable standards.

> [!question] 17. What is Continuous Integration (CI)?
> A development practice where developers frequently integrate their code into a shared repository (multiple times a day). Each integration can then be verified by an automated build and automated tests, allowing teams to detect problems early.
